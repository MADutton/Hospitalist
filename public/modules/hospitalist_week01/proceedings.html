ABVP Hospitalist Fellowship — Week 1 Proceedings

Mockup module for DABVP-CF and DABVP-Feline • Topic: Hospitalist Identity & Scope

Learning Objectives

  • Define hospitalist scope distinct from emergency practice and primary ICU case ownership.
  • Describe the time-horizon model (minutes→hours vs hours→days).
  • List core hospitalist competencies: prioritization, monitoring, escalation thresholds, and documentation clarity.
  • Identify common inpatient failure points and how to prevent them.

1. Definition: Veterinary Hospitalist

A veterinary hospitalist is an advanced clinician who owns the longitudinal management of hospitalized patients, typically across hours to days. The hospitalist’s role is to maintain continuity of care, prevent deterioration, coordinate consults, and optimize transitions (ER-to-ward, ICU-to-step-down, and ward-to-discharge).

2. Scope Boundaries

  • Included: inpatient medical management, ward-level oxygen/fluids/electrolytes, antimicrobial stewardship, pain management planning, step-down planning, discharge optimization, monitoring protocols.
  • Excluded: primary ER triage responsibility; primary ICU ownership requiring ventilator management, vasopressor titration, and advanced organ support as the main treating service.
  • Core hospitalist skill: timely recognition of when ward care is no longer appropriate and escalation is required.

3. Time Horizon Model

  • Emergency practice: minutes → hours; rapid stabilization and disposition decisions.
  • Hospitalist practice: hours → days; trend recognition, iatrogenic harm prevention, and structured reassessment.
  • Critical care: minutes → hours with continuous high-intensity monitoring; advanced organ support.

4. Hospitalist Mastery Behaviors

  • Produces a prioritized problem list that reflects near-term risk.
  • Creates an executable monitoring plan with objective deterioration triggers.
  • Updates plans based on trends (RR, mentation, weight, I/O, electrolytes), not isolated datapoints.
  • Documents succinctly so cross-coverage clinicians can act safely.

5. Common Failure Points

  • Trend blindness: “stable” label prevents reassessment.
  • Fluid creep and unrecognized weight gain, especially in CKD/CHF cats.
  • Electrolyte drift (K/Na) not anticipated or corrected.
  • Handoff gaps: missing monitoring plan, missing escalation threshold.

Documentation One-Liner

If the overnight team reads only one paragraph, they should know: the top problems, the monitoring plan, and the trigger to escalate.
